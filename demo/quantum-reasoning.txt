[2026-06-16 14:00:05] assistant: This is one of the highest-stakes bets in the field right now. Let's break it down with actual numbers and the state of the evidence as of mid-2026. I'll treat this as a decision problem with claims, evidence, and open fractures.

First, the core theoretical pitch for topological protection (Kitaev 2003, Freedman et al. 2003, Nayak et al. 2008 reviews):

In a system with non-Abelian anyons (e.g., Ising anyons from Majorana zero modes in 1D topological superconductors or Fibonacci anyons in certain fractional quantum Hall states or engineered systems), information is stored non-locally in the degenerate ground state manifold. Local errors cannot change the topological sector without closing the gap or creating a pair of anyons and braiding them. Braiding implements Clifford gates (for Ising) or universal computation (for Fibonacci) in a way that is protected by topology. The error rate for the logical qubit is exponentially suppressed in the separation of the anyons and the gap, not linearly in the physical error rate.

The famous threshold theorem still applies, but the *physical* error rate you can tolerate before needing active correction can be much higher, and the overhead for magic state distillation or gate implementation can be lower in the asymptotic limit.

Now the experimental reality check, 2026 edition:

Superconducting qubits (Google Willow, IBM, Rigetti, etc.):
- Physical two-qubit gate errors: best devices ~0.1-0.5% (down from ~1% a few years ago).
- Surface code logical error rate scaling: Google's 2023-2025 work showed below-threshold behavior. For distance-5 to distance-7 codes, they are seeing logical errors in the 10^-3 to 10^-4 range per cycle in some runs, with clear suppression as distance increases, but still far from the 10^-10 or better needed for useful algorithms without insane overhead.
- The threshold for surface codes is roughly 1% for circuit-level noise (Preskill, Fowler et al.). We're close or slightly below in the best labs, but the constant factors, crosstalk, and leakage are brutal when scaling to millions of qubits.

Trapped ions (IonQ, Quantinuum, Alpine Quantum):
- Gate errors often <0.1%, sometimes 0.01% for single-qubit.
- Better coherence, but slower gates and harder to scale ion number without shuttling or photonic interconnects.
- They have demonstrated small logical qubits with very good fidelity, but the scaling curve is different.

Topological / Majorana platforms (the controversial one):
- The 2010s-early 2020s "Majorana" signals in InAs/Al nanowires (Kouwenhoven group, Microsoft, etc.) have had multiple high-profile claims followed by retractions, alternative explanations (Andreev bound states, disorder-induced states), or inability to demonstrate the key braiding statistics or non-local parity.
- As of 2025-2026, there are new platforms: 2D materials, proximitized topological insulators, iron-based superconductors, and better-controlled hybrid devices. Some groups (e.g., certain European and Chinese collaborations) are claiming cleaner zero-bias peaks and even preliminary parity measurements.
- However, the community is still extremely cautious. No unambiguous demonstration of non-Abelian statistics or braiding that survives all alternative explanations has been universally accepted yet.
- Theoretical requirement: you need a topological gap much larger than temperature and disorder broadening, and the ability to move the anyons without exciting quasiparticles. Current materials have gaps in the 100-300 μeV range at best – marginal for dilution fridge temperatures.

The overhead comparison is where it gets quantitative k:

For surface codes to reach logical error ~10^-12 (needed for Shor or good chemistry), estimates (Fowler, Martinis, etc.) are often 10^3 to 10^4 physical qubits per logical qubit, plus massive classical decoding overhead. Topological codes can in principle have much lower overhead because the protection is "built in" and some gates are topologically protected.

But: most "topological" proposals still need some active correction for the non-protected parts, and creating and braiding the anyons at scale is an enormous materials + fabrication challenge.

Recent theoretical progress that changes the picture:
- Better understanding of "anyonic" modes in conventional systems (e.g., parafermions, higher-genus codes).
- Hybrid approaches: use conventional qubits for fast gates + topological for memory.
- Quantum gravity / holography analogs (e.g., SYK model simulations, black hole inspired error correction) suggesting that certain highly entangled codes might have better properties than we thought.
- Experimental analog gravity in condensed matter systems giving hints about information protection.

Uncertainties and fractures in the claims:
- Claim A (topological optimists): "We just need better materials and we'll get intrinsic protection that beats everything else by orders of magnitude." Fracture: The experimental track record on Majoranas has been poor for a decade. Many "signals" turned out to be mundane. The gap vs. temperature problem is fundamental in real materials.
- Claim B (conventional QEC people): "Surface codes + better qubits will get us there by 2030-2035 with enough engineering." Fracture: The overhead numbers are still terrifying for anything beyond toy algorithms, and leakage, crosstalk, and cosmic rays become showstoppers at scale.
- Claim C: "We'll need both – topological for long-term memory, active correction for gates." This is the compromise position, but it requires solving *both* hard problems.
- Open fracture: We still don't have a clear experimental smoking gun for non-Abelian anyons in a platform that can be scaled to hundreds of them. Until we do, topological QC remains a beautiful theory with a checkered experimental history.

My current synthesized position (with confidence):
- For near-term (next 5-7 years) practical advantage or error-corrected logical qubits useful for small algorithms: conventional + surface codes (or LDPC codes, which are having a renaissance) on the best available hardware is the higher-probability path. The engineering is hard but the physics is "solved" in principle.
- Topological protection is still the most elegant long-term solution if (and this is a big if) we can realize stable, braidable anyons in a material system. The risk/reward is asymmetric: if it works, the payoff is huge (much lower overhead, more robust). But the probability of it working at scale in the next decade is, in my current estimate, <30% based on the experimental literature.
- Hybrid + better theory (leveraging lessons from quantum gravity analogs for code design) is probably the smartest place to allocate marginal effort.

What would change my mind strongly:
- A clean, reproducible demonstration of braiding statistics with fidelity high enough that the topological gap is not the limiting factor.
- A theoretical breakthrough showing that certain conventional codes can achieve topological-like protection without the materials problem.
- New experimental platforms (e.g., from the 2D material or cold-atom communities) that suddenly show large, clean gaps.

This is not a pure physics question anymore – it's physics + materials science + systems engineering + probability assessment under deep uncertainty.
